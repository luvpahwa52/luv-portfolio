## TL;DR

Most RAG tutorials end at "the chatbot answers questions!" That's not done — that's just the start. Without evaluation, you can't tell if your changes are improvements or regressions. Here's the lightweight evaluation setup I wish I'd built from day one.

## Why "It Works on My Examples" Isn't Enough

Every RAG developer goes through the same trap:

1. Build chatbot
2. Try 5 example questions
3. Tweak prompt because answer #3 wasn't great
4. Try same 5 questions again
5. Ship

The problem: you have **no idea** if your tweak helped questions you didn't test. Maybe answer #3 improved but answers #1 and #2 silently got worse. Without a test set, every change is a gamble.

## The Three Things to Measure

RAG evaluation breaks into three layers:

### 1. Retrieval Quality

Did the retriever surface the right documents? This is independent of the LLM.

**Metrics:**

- **Recall@k** — of the relevant docs, how many are in top-k?
- **MRR (Mean Reciprocal Rank)** — how high up is the first relevant doc?
- **Hit Rate** — was at least one relevant doc retrieved?

### 2. Answer Quality

Given the retrieved docs, did the LLM produce a good answer?

**Metrics:**

- **Faithfulness** — does the answer stick to the retrieved context, or hallucinate?
- **Answer relevance** — does it actually address the question?
- **Completeness** — does it cover the full answer or just part?

### 3. End-to-End Quality

The user's actual experience. Sometimes retrieval and answer both look fine in isolation but the combined output is bad.

## Building a Lightweight Eval Set

You don't need 1000 examples to start. **20 well-chosen examples beat 200 random ones.**

Categorize them deliberately:

- 30% common cases (the questions users actually ask)
- 30% edge cases (exact codes, numbers, names)
- 20% multi-doc (answer requires combining sources)
- 20% adversarial (questions your bot probably fails on)

## LLM-as-Judge for Answer Quality

Manually grading answers doesn't scale. LLM-as-judge does. Use a **stronger model** as the judge than the one being evaluated. Use a **different model family** if possible to reduce bias.

## The Workflow That Saved My Sanity

Concretely:

1. Snapshot current metrics as baseline
2. Make one change (new chunking strategy, different model, new prompt)
3. Run full eval
4. Compare each metric — if anything dropped > 5%, investigate why
5. If everything held or improved, ship the change

Without this, I was making changes in the dark. With it, I could confidently iterate.

## Common Pitfalls

### Evaluating only on easy questions

If all your eval examples are softballs, you'll feel great but your bot will fail on real users. Include questions you **expect** to fail.

### Letting eval set leak into prompts

If you keep tuning prompts using examples from your eval set, you're overfitting. Hold out a portion as "test set" that you only check periodically.

### Trusting a single number

A single "quality score" hides regressions. One category getting worse while another improves can cancel out. Always look at per-category breakdowns.

## The Honest Truth

Building a RAG system that demos well is easy. Building one you can confidently improve over time requires evaluation infrastructure. The 2 days I spent building eval saved weeks of "is this actually better?" guesswork.

## Takeaways

- 🎯 **No eval = no real iteration** — you're just guessing
- 📏 **Measure retrieval and answer separately** — different failure modes
- ⚖️ **LLM-as-judge scales** when manual grading doesn't
- 📊 **20 good examples beat 200 random ones**
- 🔬 **Hold out a test set** you don't tune against
